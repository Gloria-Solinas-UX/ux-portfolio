Too Good To Go:
Redesigning Trust & Sustainability

01/
Research & Insights ■

Overview

This is a self-directed project where I applied a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative data from surveys with qualitative insights from focus groups, competitor analysis, and user personas to inform the redesign strategy.

This combination provided a holistic understanding of users — not only what they do, but why they make certain decisions when using Too Good To Go.

Focus Groups

To understand user perceptions, I conducted a focus group with both first-time and regular Too Good To Go users. Participants consistently raised concerns about trust and sustainability, saying that not knowing what’s inside the surprise bags often made them hesitate to purchase.

During the session, participants compared different rating systems, including Too Good To Go’s current star-based reviews. This discussion helped uncover how varied feedback formats (e.g., text reviews, images, and user profiles) shape trust and influence decision-making.

Surveys

Building on the user persona insights, the survey targeted individuals within Too Good To Go’s primary audience — mainly young adults and students aged 18–24 who use the app to save money and reduce food waste.

The survey provided valuable data to identify recurring pain points and behavioral patterns. Most respondents reported using the app occasionally, primarily for budget-friendly meals and to support sustainable practices.

User Persona

I synthesised both qualitative and quantitative insights into a single persona. This persona was developed from focus group findings, complemented by user reviews, reports, and industry data, ensuring it accurately reflects Too Good To Go users’ motivations, goals, and frustrations.

Research Summary
  • Users hesitate due to lack of photos & vague reviews
  • Trust depends on recency + consistency
  • Users rely on “safe choice” behaviour
  • Transparency directly influences confidence

02/
User Journey ■

Current User Journey

To identify where confidence breaks down, I mapped John's end-to-end journey across six stages. The biggest hesitation happens between Consider → Evaluate due to missing photos, vague reviews, and unclear value.

03/
Ideation ■

Early Concepts

To explore solutions quickly, I used sketching, Crazy 8s, and low-fidelity wireframes.These helped me translate the key insights (missing photos, unclear value, inconsistent reviews, and trust gaps) into early concepts.

  • Photo-first bag previews (typical items + “based on last 10 orders”)
  • Vendor reliability indicators (consistency score, verified badge)
  • Structured review layouts (recent, photo-only, value filters)

From these, I selected the strongest direction: Make transparency visible across the decision-making flow.

04/
Visual Design ■

Colour Scheme & Typography

The visual design stage focused on aligning Too Good To Go’s digital experience with its sustainability mission. I refined the colour system and typography to improve trust, accessibility, and energy efficiency while preserving brand recognition.

Instead of introducing new hues, I reorganised Too Good To Go’s original palette into a darker, low-energy interface. Replacing bright white backgrounds with deeper greens improves visual comfort, supports lower energy consumption on OLED screens, and maintains AAA accessibility (7.3:1 contrast between #00615F and #FFFFFF).

To ensure consistent readability and reduce unnecessary font downloads, I replaced the custom Work Sans font with the system default Arial. This aligns the app with platform-native typography and improves performance across devices.

05/
Testing & Iteration ■

Key Insights

I tested the mid-fidelity prototype with 5 users to evaluate whether the redesigned transparency features reduced hesitation when choosing a surprise bag. Here's what I found out:

  • Users wanted clearer photo context: They liked seeing food photos but couldn’t tell if they were recent or representative.
  • Vendor reliability badge went unnoticed: Users struggled to find it, so it didn’t influence trust early enough.
  • Review section felt overwhelming: Users wanted photo-first reviews and clearer summaries to make faster decisions.

Outcome

The redesign improved user confidence and reduced hesitation during key decision-making moments - 4 out of 5 users reported feeling more confident purchasing a surprise bag after the redesign.
