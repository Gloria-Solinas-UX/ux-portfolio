HEAPS RESEARCH

01/
Research & Content ■

Overview

This was a collaborative project where I designed and developed a research website for an academic leader at Manchester Metropolitan University. I led UX, visual design, accessibility, and front-end development to turn complex research material into a clear, user-friendly digital platform.

The site will serve as a central hub for public engagement, offering clear navigation, responsive design, and concise content tailored for academic, community, and policy audiences.

User Persona Summary

The website primarily serves academics, researchers, and practitioners who work in education inequality, social justice, and policy. Users like Dr. Mokoena need quick access to clear, theoretically grounded research without jargon. They value well-organised content, credible citations and resources they can use in teaching, policy briefs, and funding decisions.

Key User Needs

  • Clear summaries of the project’s objectives, key findings, and conceptual frameworks
  • A clean, uncluttered layout that saves time
  • Authors bios and contact details for collaboration
  • Trustworthy, academically rigorous information

User Challenges

  • Limited time to sift through dense academic content
  • Difficulty navigating cluttered or poorly structured research sites
  • Need for research that is both theoretically sound and practically relevant

Relevance

These insights shaped the website’s information architecture, layout decisions, and accessibility features. The site prioritises clarity, easy navigation, and academic credibility, ensuring users can quickly understand the research, explore findings, and access resources without friction.

02/
Information Architecture ■

Overview

To create a clear and intuitive structure for the research website, I developed a sitemap that organises content into logical sections aligned with user needs and the project’s academic goals. This helped ensure the site was easy to navigate, reduced cognitive load, and supported quick access to key findings, publications, and resources.

03/
Ideation ■

Overview

I began the project with quick low-fidelity sketches to explore layout ideas, navigation patterns, and how best to present research objectives, findings, and resources. At this stage, I was still evaluating the feasibility of certain ideas due to WordPress constraints, plugin limitations, and the structure of the CMS.

Sketching allowed me to explore concepts freely while keeping technical limitations in mind, helping me identify what could be realistically implemented and what needed to be simplified or adapted.

These early sketches became the blueprint for the final build, guiding the information architecture, hierarchy, and overall reading flow. Moving directly from sketches into coded layouts ensured the design remained both practical and aligned with the WordPress environment we were working within.

04/
Visual Design ■

Colour & Typography

The visual identity uses an earthy, South African–inspired palette to communicate themes of equity, resilience, and transformation. Dark Moss Green conveys growth and opportunity, Almond Cream brings warmth and dignity, and Sunset Dust reflects the lived realities and environments central to the research.

The UI was intentionally designed to emphasise credibility, readability, and clarity, enabling users to engage with complex research without visual distraction. To maintain accessibility, all colour combinations were tested using WCAG contrast tools to ensure compliant ratios across text, backgrounds, and interactive elements.

A modern serif typeface reinforces the academic tone of the project, while a clean sans-serif supports legibility in longer passages. Together, the colours and typography create a visual system that is professional, empathetic, and aligned with the project’s mission to present research in a clear and trustworthy way.

05/
Development & Final Design ■

WordPress

During early planning, I compared Wix and WordPress in terms of cost, flexibility, features, and security. Based on this evaluation, I recommended WordPress as the most sustainable option due to its stronger customisation capabilities, better long-term value, and more robust security ecosystem.

The final build was developed in WordPress, where I tailored page layouts and components to fit the constraints of the theme and block editor. I customised the Twenty Twenty-Five template, refined typography, and structured content so it remained clear, accessible, and easy for non-technical team members to update. This approach ensured the design was both technically feasible and fully aligned with the academic nature of the project.
